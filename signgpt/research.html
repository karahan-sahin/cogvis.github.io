SignGPT · EPSRC Programme Grant

Research

Four tightly integrated pillars spanning visual sign understanding, generative modelling, linguistic grounding, and community co-design — working toward unconstrained, bidirectional BSL–English translation.

Motivation

Why BSL Needs a New Approach

British Sign Language is the primary language of approximately 87,000 Deaf people in the United Kingdom. Despite being a recognised minority language with its own grammar, phonology, and discourse structure, BSL remains severely underserved by existing natural language technology. Most current systems either require constraining signers to a predefined vocabulary, rely on explicit gloss annotations as an intermediate representation, or operate only in the isolated-sign regime.

The core hypothesis of SignGPT is that the limitations of prior work stem from an architectural mismatch: treating sign language as a structured sequence of discrete symbols, rather than as a spatiotemporal, multi-channel signal governed by a rich generative grammar. A truly generative model — one that learns to predict the next visual token given linguistic context — can overcome these limitations.

P1
Pillar One

Visual Sign Understanding

The first pillar addresses the fundamental perceptual challenge: extracting linguistically meaningful representations from raw BSL video. A signer communicates simultaneously through hand shape (handshape), hand position relative to the body and signing space (location), direction and speed of movement (movement), palm/finger orientation, and a range of non-manual articulators — facial expression, eye gaze, mouth patterns, and head position.

Existing visual backbones were designed for action recognition and are poorly suited to this multi-channel, fine-grained signal. SignGPT will develop purpose-built video encoders that factorize the sign stream into its phonological parameters, enabling the downstream transformer to reason at the appropriate level of linguistic abstraction. Pre-training will be conducted on a newly collected, large-scale BSL corpus drawn from broadcast media and community-contributed recordings.

A key research challenge is handling the continuous, co-articulated nature of natural signing: signs blur into one another without clear boundaries, and many sign classes differ only in subtle spatial or kinematic details. We will investigate learned temporal segmentation, phoneme-level tokenisation, and self-supervised objectives derived from BSL linguistic structure.

Key Research Questions
What visual representations best capture the phonological parameters of BSL?
How can non-manual articulators be jointly modelled with manual signs?
What self-supervised objectives leverage BSL linguistic structure during pre-training?
Techniques
Video transformers Phonological tokenisation Self-supervised pre-training Pose estimation Face analysis
P2
Pillar Two

Generative Sign Language Modelling

The centrepiece of SignGPT is a novel generative predictive transformer trained to model the joint distribution over visual sign tokens and their linguistic meaning. Inspired by the success of large language models for spoken language — which learn by predicting the next token — our architecture extends this paradigm to the visual-linguistic domain of sign language.

The model operates on a discrete codebook of visual sign tokens produced by the Pillar 1 encoder. At inference time, it can be conditioned on English text to generate a sequence of sign tokens (production), or conditioned on a sign token sequence to decode English (translation). This unified architecture avoids the asymmetry of separate recognition and production systems and enables joint training on heterogeneous data sources — annotated BSL video, unannotated BSL broadcast material, and parallel text corpora.

Bidirectional training is central to our approach: a model that can produce plausible BSL from English has, by necessity, learned a generative model of BSL structure, which in turn improves translation accuracy. We will investigate scaling laws for this class of models, the effect of visual token vocabulary size, and methods for conditioning on speaker identity and register.

Key Research Questions
Can a single generative transformer achieve state-of-the-art performance on both BSL recognition and production?
How does visual token vocabulary size affect translation quality and production diversity?
What scaling laws apply to sign language generative models?
Techniques
Generative transformers Visual tokenisation (VQ-VAE) Seq2Seq pre-training Cross-modal alignment Large-scale pre-training
P3
Pillar Three

BSL Linguistics Integration

BSL is a full natural language with its own distinct grammar — it is not a manual encoding of English. Its grammatical devices include use of three-dimensional signing space to establish referents, classify predicates that indicate shape, size, and movement of objects, and a topic-comment discourse structure. Non-manual articulators carry essential grammatical information: raised eyebrows mark yes/no questions; furrowed brows mark wh-questions; backward head tilt marks rhetorical questions.

The Pillar 3 work, led by linguists at UCL DCAL, ensures that the transformer architecture is informed by — and evaluated against — this rich structural knowledge. Specific contributions include: (i) a formal computational specification of BSL spatial grammar suitable for neural grounding; (ii) annotated training data capturing classifier predicates and agreement verbs; (iii) linguistically motivated evaluation metrics that go beyond surface BLEU scores.

This pillar also addresses the question of variation within BSL — regional dialects, age-related variation, and differences between Deaf and hearing signers — ensuring that the system is robust and inclusive rather than trained to a narrow standard.

Key Research Questions
How can spatial grammar be represented in a differentiable, learnable form?
What evaluation metrics capture linguistic adequacy beyond BLEU and ROUGE?
How should the model handle dialectal variation within BSL?
Techniques
Formal grammar formalisms Annotation tooling Classifier predicate modelling Discourse structure
P4
Pillar Four

Community Co-Design & Evaluation

Technical excellence alone does not guarantee that an AI system serves the community it is intended to benefit. Pillar 4 embeds Deaf community participation throughout the programme — not as a downstream validation step, but as a co-design activity that shapes research questions, dataset construction, and evaluation criteria from the outset.

We work with the British Deaf Association (BDA), the Royal Association of the Deaf (RAD), and BSL Broadcasting Trust (BSLBT / LumoTV) to co-create recording protocols, annotation guidelines, and test sets that reflect the diversity of BSL usage. An Advisory Board including representatives from these organisations, BBC R&D, Microsoft Munich, and leading European sign language research groups provides programme-level oversight and strategic input.

Evaluation extends beyond automatic metrics to structured Deaf-user studies measuring intelligibility, naturalness, and communicative adequacy of system outputs. Community feedback is integrated into each major development cycle, creating a continuous co-design loop between researchers and end users.

Key Research Questions
How should Deaf community members be meaningfully included at each stage of the research cycle?
What user-centred evaluation protocols best measure real-world communicative utility?
How can dataset collection be designed to be inclusive of BSL's linguistic diversity?

Research Roadmap

Key milestones across the six-year programme, structured around the four research pillars.

Programme Phases
Phase I (Y1–2): Foundations & corpus collection
Phase II (Y3–4): Model training & benchmarks
Phase III (Y5–6): Integration & pilot deployment
Completion (2031): Legacy & policy outputs
2025 — Programme Launch
Infrastructure & team onboarding
All three partner sites activated; initial PhD and postdoctoral recruitment; community partnership agreements established with BDA, RAD, and BSLBT; corpus collection protocols agreed with linguistic advisors.
2026 — Phase I Milestones
BSL corpus v1 & visual backbone pre-training
First large-scale BSL corpus release; phonological tokeniser trained and benchmarked; Pillar 3 annotation schema finalised; community co-design workshops completed across all Deaf organisation partners.
2027–2028 — Phase II Milestones
Generative transformer training & evaluation benchmarks
First generation of the SignGPT transformer trained at scale; new BSL translation and production benchmarks released; Deaf-user evaluation study 1 conducted; Pillar 3 linguistic evaluation framework published.
2029–2030 — Phase III Milestones
End-to-end system & community pilots
Bidirectional BSL–English system integrated into pilot accessibility applications with BDA and BSLBT; production avatar system released; open-source model weights and toolkit published.
2031 — Completion
Legacy outputs, open datasets & policy recommendations
Final programme report; open release of all datasets, benchmarks, and model weights; policy white paper on AI for Deaf accessibility; roadmap for continued community-led development beyond the grant.

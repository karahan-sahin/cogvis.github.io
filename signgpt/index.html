Programme Overview

Abstract

SignGPT is a six-year EPSRC Programme Grant developing an AI-powered translation system capable of unconstrained, bidirectional translation between British Sign Language (BSL) and English. The project unites world-leading expertise in computer vision (CVSSP, University of Surrey), large-scale machine learning (VGG, University of Oxford), and BSL linguistics (DCAL, UCL), in close partnership with British Deaf organisations.

Unlike previous approaches constrained to predefined vocabularies or gloss-mediated pipelines, SignGPT targets full linguistic fluency — handling the spatial grammar, non-manual features (facial expression, mouthing, eyegaze), classifier predicates, and rich iconicity of BSL in real-world, conversational settings. The centrepiece is a novel generative predictive transformer architecture that jointly models visual sign production and linguistic structure at scale, trained on a newly collected large-scale BSL corpus. Community co-design and rigorous Deaf-user evaluation are embedded throughout.

Programme Structure

Research Pillars

Four tightly integrated workstreams spanning visual computing, generative modelling, linguistics, and community co-design.

Full Research Details
Computer Vision NLP Linguistics Co-Design
P1
Pillar One
Visual Sign Understanding

Scalable video encoders capturing hand shape, movement, location, orientation, and non-manual features — facial expression, eye gaze, mouthing — in continuous, unconstrained BSL video. Includes large-scale pre-training on a new BSL corpus.

Read more → Video Transformers Self-Supervised Pre-Training
P2
Pillar Two
Generative Sign Language Modelling

The first large-scale generative predictive transformer for BSL — a unified model learning the joint distribution over visual tokens and linguistic structure, enabling both recognition and production in a single architecture.

Read more → Generative Transformers VQ-VAE Tokenisation
P3
Pillar Three
BSL Linguistics Integration

Grounding the transformer in rigorous BSL linguistic theory: spatial grammar, classifier predicates, topic-comment structure, and discourse-level phenomena, in close collaboration with UCL DCAL linguists.

Read more → Spatial Grammar Linguistic Evaluation
P4
Pillar Four
Community Co-Design & Evaluation

Co-designing datasets, benchmarks, and evaluation protocols with Deaf organisations — BDA, RAD, BSLBT — to ensure cultural appropriateness, real-world validity, and measurable accessibility impact across all programme outputs.

Read more → BDA RAD BSLBT
